WILCOXON-MANN-WHITNEY TEST (MANNWHITNEY U TEST, WILCOXON RANK SUM TEST)

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

When comparing 2 groups on an ordinal or nonnormally distributed continuous outcome variable, the 2-sample t test is usually not appropriate.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

This test is akin to a t test on the ranks of the data.33

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

Data are ranked from smallest to largest while ignoring group assignment, then the groups are compared on the mean rank (accounting for ties).34

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

the null hypothesis for the Mann-Whitney test is Ho: P(X > Y) = .5.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

In other words, the probability that a randomly sampled patient from group 1 has a higher value than a randomly sampled patient from group 2 is equal to .50, or a coin flip.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

1. Random samples are for 2 populations.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

2. Outcomes are independent within samples.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

3. Measurement scale is at least ordinal.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

4. Variance of outcome is the same for each group.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

The Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney test assesses whether there is a location or pattern shift between the 2 populations of interest.35

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

If the null is rejected, one concludes that values of one group tend to be higher than the other group.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

Exemplary uses of this test might be to compare randomized or propensity-matched groups on an ordinal outcome such a numerical rating scale pain score, or a skewed (nonnormally distributed) variable like opioid consumption.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

An extension of the Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney test for comparing more than 2 groups is the Kruskal-Wallis test.20,33,34

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

When reporting Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney test results, it is important to avoid stating that a significant result implies that there is evidence that the medians differ or that they do not differ.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

The test does not explicitly compare medians, but rather a general location or pattern shift.35

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

In fact, it is quite possible to have a valid statistically significant Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney result when the observed medians are exactly the same.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

Results for the Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney can be sufficiently reported as the median (interquartile range) for each group, along with the median difference between groups and its confidence interval, calculated using the Hodges-Lehman estimator.36

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

It is also appropriate to report the observed MannWhitney probability, P(X > Y), simply called “p.”

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

It can be even more informative to report the odds for this probability, or p/(1 − p), which is called the Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney odds.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

A confidence interval for the Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney odds should also be reported.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

Further details, including sample size calculations and full examples, are provided in the excellent article by Divine et al.37

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

The authors reported that compared to placebo, the median intraoperative opioid use was reduced in the dexmedetomidine group but not at 24 hours postoperatively.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

As compared to placebo, no difference in median pain scores, as measured by an 11-point discrete scale, was observed at 24 and at 48 hours postoperatively in the dexmedetomidine group.38

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

As mentioned above, while this was an appropriate use of the Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney test, the correct interpretation would be that values of intraoperative opioid use were reduced (not that the median per se was reduced) in 1 group versus the other.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

Naik et al38 could also have reported the median difference and its confidence interval, as well as the Wilcoxon-Mann-Whitney probability and odds.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

WILCOXON SIGNED-RANK TEST

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

When making paired comparisons on data that are ordinal, or continuous but nonnormally distributed, the Wilcoxon signed-rank test can be used.20,33,34,39

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

It is an alternative to the paired t test when the paired differences do not appear to be normally distributed.34

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

Typical uses of this test would be comparing patients from before to after an intervention or procedure, such as surgery.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

Results could best be reported as the median difference and confidence interval for the difference.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

Technically, it is used to assess whether 2 dependent samples were selected from the populations having the same distribution.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

1. Data are paired and come from the same population.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

2. Each pair is chosen randomly and independently.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

3. The data are at least ordinal.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

In their study of the influence of nociceptive stimulation on the analgesia nociception index (ANI) during propofol–remifentanil anesthesia, Gruenewald et al40 applied a Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

Specifically, the primary outcome variables of the ANI and the surgical pleth index were obtained in 25 patients before and after nociceptive stimulation.

Highlight (yellow) - Page 4

Each study subject thus provided paired data for the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.

Highlight (orange) - Page 4

The ANI and surgical pleth index were significantly changed by the insertion of a laryngeal mask airway and the delivery of neuromuscular tetanic stimulation.40
